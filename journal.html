A Working-Class Man in a Middle-Class Industry.

Entering the TA 101 for the first time, my nervous system felt as though it was in meltdown; I was not okay. I made a beeline for the corner.

Read Entry →

Entering the TA 101 for the first time, my nervous system felt as though it was in meltdown; I was not okay. I made a beeline for the corner. Unfortunately for me, that corner was at the front where the tutors present. ‘Great,’ I thought, ‘no hiding place here.’

I was not okay, but everyone else was okay. My thoughts for that morning were: there are going to be trained therapists, counsellors, healthcare professionals, etc. What am I doing here, a factory worker from Rotherham? ‘I am never going to succeed at this,’ I said to myself. A working-class man from a working-class town—what a massive discount to myself.

Making new connections has traditionally been awkward for me, as I have a clear ‘Don’t be close’ and ‘Don’t trust’ injunction. I always have a lot to say, but I just don’t have the courage to say so. Most of the time, I have an overwhelming surge of emotions if I even think about talking during check-in/out and during process group, which makes me feel it is easier not to say anything.

The guilt I then place on myself for not speaking up has been worse than the silence when I’ve finished the training on some weekends. There is just a sense that what I have to say is not valid or worthwhile—do I even make sense to myself or to others? So, the importance of leaning into the difficult feeling of vulnerability is something that I must carry on doing, moving forward on my journey into psychotherapy.

Making Sense of the Inner Dialogue.

Read Entry →

I have always been aware of the word "ego," but the terminology of ‘ego state’ was not in my frame of reference until the last five years. I was introduced to the first-order structural model (Berne, 1972), and suddenly, my inner dialogue began to make much more sense. This model, often referred to as the three ego state model or the PAC model, divides the ego into three separate states: Parent, Adult, and Child.

As I began to map out my own ego states, I realised how deeply they are shaped by the outside world—particularly the Parent state, which absorbs the rules and expectations of society. This is where intersectionality enters the frame. During my studies, it became apparent that I need to actively consider this; it is something I hadn't traditionally reflected on in my everyday life, or if I did, it remained completely in my unconscious.

As a working-class male entering a middle-class industry dominated by females, I need to consider my own intersectional context in order to truly understand my client’s experience. I have had to think critically about gender roles and society’s expectations. I do not believe that women should be solely responsible for the caregiver roles within a family, but this is a framework I was raised around, and I must be aware that it might not be the experience or belief of the next client that walks through my door.

Subtle Movements Toward Choice and Presence.

Read Entry →

When I think about the formation of script, what comes to mind first are the old messages I carried as a kid. In TA, we call these injunctions. Through deconfusion, these messages can be brought into Adult awareness, allowing for new meaning-making and, ultimately, redecision.

When my self-criticism appears, I am starting to notice and can name it internally, and in turn, regulate myself rather than act from it. These shifts reflect the core of IC [Intrapsychic Change]: not dramatic transformation, but subtle movements toward choice, grounding, and presence.

At times, I wonder whether the intensity of my reactions comes from trauma, neurodivergence, or simply the way I am wired. But I am beginning to realise that the label does not matter; it is more the meaning I make of who I am. Quirks, sensitivities, control, rigidity, structure, triggers—and working with them—forms the basis of my development as an integrative TA practitioner.
