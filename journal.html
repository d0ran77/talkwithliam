A Working-Class Man in a Middle-Class Industry.

Entering the TA 101 for the first time, my nervous system felt as though it was in meltdown; I was not okay. I made a beeline for the corner.

Read Entry →

Entering the TA 101 for the first time, my nervous system felt as though it was in meltdown; I was not okay. I made a beeline for the corner. Unfortunately for me, that corner was at the front where the tutors present. ‘Great,’ I thought, ‘no hiding place here.’

I was not okay, but everyone else was okay. My thoughts for that morning were clear: there are going to be trained therapists, counsellors, and healthcare professionals here. What am I doing here, a factory worker from Rotherham? ‘I am never going to succeed at this,’ I said to myself. A working-class man from a working-class town, what a massive discount to myself.

Making new connections has traditionally been awkward for me, as I have a clear ‘Don’t be close’ and ‘Don’t trust’ injunction. I always have a lot to say, but I just don’t have the courage to say so. Most of the time, I have an overwhelming surge of emotions if I even think about talking during check-in and during process group, which makes me feel it is easier not to say anything.

The guilt I then place on myself for not speaking up has been worse than the silence when I have finished the training on some weekends. There is just a sense that what I have to say is not valid or worthwhile. Do I even make sense to myself or to others? So, the importance of leaning into the difficult feeling of vulnerability is something that I must carry on doing as I move forward on my journey into psychotherapy.

Silence of the Volcano: From Reactivity to Clarity.

Exploring how early environments shape our defensive walls, and how therapy helps us find a quiet presence.

Read Entry →

When reflecting on my own process, I often consider the roots of impatience and frustration. For a long time, I carried a profound sense of anger. It is not healthy for an adult to walk around with that level of tension, and I can only imagine how confusing it must have been when I was younger.

I frequently use the analogy of a volcano. It felt like a burning reactivity coming from deep within that would suddenly erupt. I could feel it rising through my body, culminating in what felt like a physical explosion in my mind. There was no 'flight' response back then. It was entirely 'fight', because I had made an unconscious decision to protect myself.

"The volcano was created as a superpower, a way for a child to build a wall against an unpredictable world."

In her work on script formation, Clarkson (1991) notes that a child will often absorb the tension of a difficult environment into themselves. When an early environment feels chaotic, a child adapts to survive it. In my own experience, that adaptation looked like a rigid, protective baseline of anger.

Clarkson suggests that children internalise these volatile emotions until they become a structural part of how they navigate the world. The anger simply becomes a familiar shield.

Recently, however, through the process of my own personal therapy, I have been experiencing a new sensation. It is hard to put into words, but it feels remarkably like freedom. Where there was once constant noise, there is now a quiet space for the present. It is a state of simply being in the moment without the overwhelming internal dialogue or the need to defend against a threat that is no longer there. It feels relaxing, exciting, and leaves me deeply curious about the therapeutic process.

Subtle Movements Toward Choice and Presence.

At times, I wonder whether the intensity of my reactions comes from trauma, neurodivergence, or simply the way I am wired. But I am beginning to realise that the label does not matter.

Read Entry →

When I think about the formation of script, what comes to mind first are the old messages I carried as a kid. In TA, we call these injunctions. Through deconfusion, these messages can be brought into Adult awareness, allowing for new meaning-making and, ultimately, redecision.

When my self-criticism appears, I am starting to notice and can name it internally, and in turn, regulate myself rather than act from it. These shifts reflect the core of IC [Intrapsychic Change]: not dramatic transformation, but subtle movements toward choice, grounding, and presence.

At times, I wonder whether the intensity of my reactions comes from trauma, neurodivergence, or simply the way I am wired. But I am beginning to realise that the label does not matter. It is more the meaning I make of who I am. Quirks, sensitivities, control, rigidity, structure, triggers, and working with them, forms the basis of my development as an integrative TA practitioner.
